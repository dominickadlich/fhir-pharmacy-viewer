## What I learned

medicationReference vs. medicationCodeableConcept is not a minor implementation detail, it's a fundamental data modeling decision that affects every medication parser you write.

In the FHIR R4 spec, MedicationRequest represents the medication as either an inline coded concept (medicationCodeableConcept) or a reference to a separate Medication resource (medicationReference). Epic uses both. The open SMART Health IT server tends toward medicationCodeableConcept with RxNorm codes. Epic's production sandbox leans toward medicationReference, which offloads the medication details to a linked resource.

The more important discovery here is the clinical depth that medicationReference carries. This single request returned:

- reasonCode — the indication, coded in SNOMED-CT, ICD-9, and ICD-10 simultaneously. This patient's YAZ was prescribed for PCOS, and that context is attached directly to the order.
- dosageInstruction — the full sig: route, frequency, dose quantity, and patient-facing instructions.
- courseOfTherapyType — continuous long-term therapy, distinguishing this from an acute or as-needed prescription.
- dispenseRequest — quantity (28 tablets), days supply (28 days), and refills authorized (12 repeats).

This is the data model that will drive the clinical logic layer of the pharmacy viewer. The indication, the dispensing contract, and the therapy duration are all accessible without a secondary query.

## LinkedIn Post
Pulled my first fully populated MedicationRequest from Epic's sandbox this week, and the data structure is worth documenting.

The FHIR spec gives implementers two ways to represent a medication on a MedicationRequest: inline as a medicationCodeableConcept with a coded drug identifier, or as a medicationReference pointing to a separate Medication resource. Epic uses both depending on the record. If your parser only handles one pattern, you'll get undefined on the other — silently, with no error.

More interesting than the parsing challenge is what medicationReference actually contains. A single request returned the drug name, dose, route, and sig — expected. But it also returned the indication coded in SNOMED-CT and ICD-10, the course of therapy type (continuous long-term), and the full dispense contract: quantity, days supply, and authorized refills.

From a pharmacy informatics standpoint, that's a meaningful amount of clinical context in a single FHIR resource. The indication alone changes how a pharmacist interprets the prescription. Building a viewer that surfaces all of it — not just the drug name and status — is the point of this project.
